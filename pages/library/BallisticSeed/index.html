Dispersal Structure - Ballistic Seed

Inspiration

The Himalayan balsam (Impatiens glandulifera) uses explosive seed pods for dispersal. Each pod contains 4-16 seeds and, when disturbed, bursts open, propelling seeds up to 7 meters away. The pods split along weak lines, ejecting the seeds with force. These seeds are often unintentionally spread by humans or small animals, and water also plays a significant role in dispersal, as the buoyant seeds can float long distances on currents, especially during floods. Balsam plants produce around 700-800 seeds per year, and the seeds have a high germination rate of about 80%. However, their seeds do not persist in the soil for more than 18 months, so control efforts can be effective with consistent management over 2-3 years, as long as new seeds aren’t introduced. This explosive dispersal strategy makes the balsam plant a highly effective and aggressive colonizer.

How it Works

The spring-loaded seed pods bend when the temperature drops, and as humidity rises, the constraints on the pods are released, causing the energy stored within them to discharge instantly, propelling the seeds outward.

Sample Conditions:

  • Temperature change from 35°C to 25°C
  • Humidity change from 30% to 100%

Sample Parameters:

  • Wood thickness: 0.2 mm and 0.5 mm；
  • Strips thickness: 0.2 mm；
  • Strips interval: 1.6 mm；
  • Angle difference: 90˚；
  • Driven factors: Thermal and Hygro
